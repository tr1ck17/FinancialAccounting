Current Liabilities
	Liabilities are obligations resulting from past transactions or events that require a company to pay money, provide goods, or perform services in the future
	Current liabilities are due within the next year or normal operating cycle, whichever is longer

Accounts Payable and Notes Payable
	Accounts payable
		Amounts due to short-term creditors
		Usually non-interest bearing
	Notes payable
		Promissory notes with credit periods longer than the 30 to 60 days typical with accounts payable
		Usually interest bearing

Interest Payable Example
	Pomona Corporation signed a 90-day note on November 1, for $10,000 with 6 percent annual interest in exchange for equipment.

	Nov. 1 Equipment (+A)					10,000
		 Notes Payable (+L)				   10,000

	Dec 31 Interest Expense (+E, -SE)			100
		 Interest payable (+L)				   100
		(to accrue interest expense (10,000 * 0.06 * 2/12)

	Jan. 31 Interest Expense (+E, -SE)			50
		Interest Payable (-L)				100
		Notes Payable (-L)				10,000
		   Cash (-A)					   10,150

Unearned Revenue
	Advanced payments from customers
		Recorded as a liability until the products are delivered or the service is provided
		Examples include tickets sold in advance of an event or advance payments for services

	Assume Southwest Airline sells a ticket on June 1 for travel on July 15 for $300
	June 1 Cash (+A)				300
		 Unearned Revenue (+L)			   300
	July 15	Unearned Revenue (-L)			300
		  Ticket Revenue (+R, +SE)		   300

Payroll-Related Liabilities
	Payroll expenses are a major cost for most firms
	Salaries and wages
	Amounts withheld from employees' paychecks
		Federal, state, social security, and Medicare taxes
		Health insurance, union dues, and retirement
	Payroll taxes and benefits paid by the employer
		FICA (Social Security and Medicare), federal and state unemployment taxes

Notes and Bonds
	Notes are typically repaid in equal periodic installments (monthly, quarterly, or annually) that include an amount for interest and a partial repayment of the principal
		Usually arranged with a single lender
	Bonds pay periodic interest (usually semi-annual) in addition to the principal amount at maturity
		Usually issued to a large number of buyers

Bonds Terminology
	Face Value-the amount that will be paid to the bond holder when the bond matures. Typically, each bond has a $1,000 face value
	Coupon Rate - the rate at which periodic interest will be paid on the bond, typically semiannually
	Market Rate - the rate the market uses to value the bond's future cash flows of interest and principal
		Can differ from the coupon rate stated on the bond certificate because of a delay in issuing bonds after they are printed

Bond Discounts and Premiums
Recording Bond
	To Record bond issued at face value

	Cash (+A)
	   Bonds Payable (+L)
	to record the issuance of bonds

	Bond interest expense (+E, -SE)
	   Cash (-A)
	to record the payment of semiannual interest on bonds

	Bonds payable (-L)
	   Cash (-A)
	to record the retirement of bonds

Recording Bond
	To record bond issued at a discount:

	Cash (+A)
	Discount on bonds payable (+XL, -L)
	   Bonds payable (+L)
	to record the issuance of bonds

Recording Bond
To record bond issued at a premium:
	Cash (+A)
	   Bonds payable (+L)
	   Premium on bonds payable (+XL, +L)
	to record issuance of bonds

Financial Statement Presentation
	Discount on bonds payable is a deduction from the face value of bonds payable

	Bonds payable			100,000
	Less: Discount on bonds payable	6,463
	Bonds payable, net		93,537

	Premium on bonds payable is as an addition to the face value of bonds payable:

	Bonds payable				100,000
	Add: premium on bonds payable		7,020
	Bonds payable, net			107,020

Analyzing Liabilities
	Working capital is defined as the difference between current assets and current liabilities
		In general, higher working capital is preferable and viewed as a sign of a stronger financial position
	Two measures of the adequacy of working capital are:
		Current ratio
		Quick ratio
Current Ratio
	Current assets / Current liabilities

	Historically, a current ration below 2 was considered a potential concern
	More recently, many companies successfully maintain much lower current ratios through effective management of receivables, inventory, and accounts payable

Quick Ratio
	(Cash and cash equivalents + short-term investments + accounts receivable) / current liabilities

	Cash and cash equivalents, short-term investments and accounts receivable are known as "quick assets" because they are more quickly converted into cash than other current assets such as inventory

Times-Interest-Earned Ratio

	Income before interest expense and income taxes / interest expense

	The times-interest-earned ratio provides an indication of a company's ability to make periodic interest payments to its creditors
	Many investment professionals believe the ratio should be at least in the 3.0-4.0 range
