Forming a Corporation
	A corporation is an organization authorized by the state where it is formed as a separate legal entity
	The founders of a corporation prepare and file articles of incorporation defining the basic structure of the corporation, its purpose and the amount of authorized stock
	The founders elect a Board of Directors and adopt the corporation's bylaws
	The corporation obtains the funds needed to begin operations by issuing (selling) capital stock to stockholders (owners of the corporation)

The Corporate Form - Advantages
	Separate legal entity
	Limited liability
	Transferability of ownership
	Continuity of existence
	Capital raising capability

	The ability to raise capital is primarily and the result of two of the other advantages - limited liability and transferability of ownership

The Corporate Form - Disadvantages
	Organization costs
	Taxation
	Regulation and supervision

Par Value
	The face value of each share of capital stock specified in the articles of incorporation
	Par value is often set at a trivial amount ($0.01) and is unrelated to market value or issuance price
	In some states, par value may represent
		the minimum amount that must be contributed per share
		the minimum amount of contributed capital that must remain in the corporation (legal capital)
	Accountants record the par value of stock transactions in a separate capital stock account

No-Par Value Stock
	Most states permit companies to issue stock without a par value (no-par stock)
		In this case, the company's board of directors usually sets a stated value representing the corporation's legal capital
		for accounting purposes, stated value is treated similarly to par value
Capital Stock
	Authorized Shares
		the maximum number of shares that may be issued for each class of capital stock
	Issued Shares
		shares that have been sold to stockholders
	Outstanding shares
		shares that have been issued and remain in the hands of stockholders
	Treasury Shares
		Shares that have been reacquired by the corporation and not cancelled

Common Stock
	When only one type of capital stock is issued it is called common stock. Common shareholders:
		Elect the board of directors and vote on corporate matters requiring approval of the owners
		Share in earnings distributed as dividends
		Have a residual claim on corporate assets in the event of a liquidation
	Corporations may issue more than class of common stock with dividend or voting rights

Preferred Stock
	Dividend preference
		entitled to a dividend before common stockholders
		if dividends are cumulative, then any dividends in arrears (omitted dividends in past years) must be paid prior to common shareholders receiving any dividends
	Asset distribution preference
		preference over common stockholders in liquidation

Stock Issuance
	Issuing stock with a par value
	Lester Corporation issues 100 shares of $100 par value preferred stock for $13,000 and 100 shares of no-par (and no stated value) common stock for $3,000

	Cash (+A)							13,000
	   Preferred stock (+SE)			   		   10,000
	   Paid-in capital in excess of par value - preferred stock	   3,000
	to record issuance of preferred stock

	Issuing no-par stock
	Issued 30,000 shares of no-par value common stock, stated value $5, at $8 cash per share:

	Cash (+A)							240,000
	   Common Stock (+SE)					   	   150,000

Stock Splits
	A forward stock split increases the number of outstanding shares and proportionately reduces the par (or stated) value of the stock
	A reverse stock split reduces the number of outstanding shares and increases the par value
	Stock splits do not change the balances of any stockholder equity accounts

Treasury Stock
	Shares of the company's stock that it has acquired and holds for purposes other than retiring them
		For example, to reissue as part of employee stock option plans or to use in acquiring another company
		Do not have the usual rights of outstanding shares (voting, preemptive, dividends, liquidation)
	Treasury stock is a contra-equity account
		Reduces total stockholders' equity on the balance sheet

Accounting for Treasury Stock
	Kitzer Corporation acquires 200 shares of its common stock for $15 per share

	Treasury stock (+XSE, -SE)				3,000
	   Cash (-A)						   3,000
	to record the purchase of 200 shares of treasury stock at $15 per share

	Kitzer later reissues 100 shares of its treasury stock for $20 per share

	Cash (+A)						2,000
	   Treasury Stock (-XSE, +SE)				   1,500
	   Paid-in Capital - treasury stock (+SE)		   500
	to record the sale of 100 shares of treasury stock at $20 per share

	No gain (or loss) is recognized on the income statement for transactions in the company's own stock

Cash Dividends
	Dividends are a distribution of assets or shares of stock from a corporation to its shareholders
		Dividends are not required to be paid by a corporation; however, they become a liability once they are declared
		Dividends are not considered an expense and do not appear on the income statement; instead, they are a direct reduction of retained earnings
		Cash is the most common form of dividend distribution

	Cash dividends (-SE)				1,000
	   Cash (-A)					   1,000
	to record the declaration and payment of cash dividends

Stock Dividends
	Stock dividends are accounted for as a transfer of retained earnings to contributed capital
		total stockholders' equity does not change
		The proportionate ownership of each shareholder does not change
	Small stock dividends are a distribution of < 25% of the number of shares previously outstanding
		Recorded at the market value of the shares issued
	Large stock dividends are  distribution of > 25% of the number of shares previously outstanding
		Recorded at the par value of the shares issued

Statement of Retained Earnings
	The statement of retained earnings reports
		1. the beginning balance of retained earnings
		2. items that caused retained earnings to change, and
		3. the ending balance
	Typical changes to retained earnings include net income or loss, and dividends declared

Statement of Stockholders' Equity
	Most corporations present a statement of stockholders' equity reconciling all stockholders' equity accounts for the accounting period

Analyzing Stockholders' Equity
	A financial ratio of particular interest to common stockholders is the return on equity (ROE)

	Return on equity = net income / average stockholders' equity

		this ratio measures the overall profitability of the stockholders' investment in the company

Analyzing Dividend Policy
	Some investors are primarily interested in receiving cash dividends while other investors focus on appreciation of the market value of their shares

	Dividend Yield = annual dividend per share / market price per share

		The dividend yield measures the rate of return in cash dividends from an investment in the company's common stock

	Dividend payout = annual dividend per share / earnings per share

		The dividend payout ratio measures the percentage of the net income available to common shareholders that is paid out as dividends
