Accounting for Merchandising Operations
Merchandising Operations
	Manufacturers convert raw materials and component parts into finished products
	Merchandisers buy finished products for resale to a customer
		Wholesalers buy finished products in large volumes from manufacturers and sell the product in smaller quantities to retailers
		Retailers typically buy products from wholesalers for sale to the general public

Operating Cycle of a Merchandising Firm
	Three primary steps for merchandising firms:
		Purchase merchandise (Inventory/Accounts payable or cash)
		Sell merchandise (Account receivable)
		Receive cash from customer (Cash)

Cost Flows
	Beginning Inventory + Cost of Goods Purchased
		  Cost of Goods Available for Sale
	Ending Inventory    +     Cost of Goods Sold

Inventory Systems
	Perpetual System
		Cost of goods sold is calculated after every sale
		Inventory balance is kept "perpetually" up-to-date
		Provides greater control over inventory
		Better able to determine theft or spoilage
	Periodic System
		Cost of goods sold is calculated "periodically" when a physical count of inventory is taken
		Actual balance of inventory is unknown until the periodic count

Accounting for Purchases of Merchandise Perpetual Inventory System
	Debit Inventory for the acquisition cost and either:
		Credit Cash if the purchase is for cash, or
		Credit Accounts Payable if the purchase is on account
	Kali Company purchases 200 cameras on account for resale to customers. The cost of the cameras is $42,000. The following journal entry records this transaction:
	Inventory (+A)		42,000
	 Account payable (+L)		42,000
	to record the purchase of 200 cameras for resale

Transportation Costs
	All costs incurred to get the merchandise ready for resale are included in the cost of inventory

	Transportation costs incurred by Buyer:
		When the buyer is responsible for shipping costs, it is referred to as FOB shipping point
		Kali Company pays $200 in shipping costs on the inventory purchased from seller. The following journal entry records this transaction:
	Inventory (+A)		200
	 Cash (-A)		  200
	to record shipping costs paid for 200 cameras

	Transportation costs incurred by Seller
		When the seller is responsible for shipping costs, it is referred to as FOB destination
		Malibu Company pays $300 in shipping costs to deliver the inventory to a buyer. The following journal entry records this transaction:
	Freight-out expense (+E, -SE)		300
	 Cash (-A)				 300
	to record payment for the transportation costs for goods sold

Purchase Returns and Allowances
	Purchase returns
		return of merchandise by the buyer for credit
	Purchase allowances
		reduction in the purchase price, often to induce the buyer to keep merchandise that might otherwise be returned
	Assume Kali returns 20 cameras. The cameras originally cost Kali $210 each
	Accounts Payable (-L)		4,200
	 Inventory (-A)			  4,200
	to record the return of 20 cameras

Purchase Discounts
	Sellers often extend credit to customers to increase sales
		The credit period is the maximum time given for a payment
		for example, 30 days terms will be stated as n/30
	Extending credit delays the receipt of cash for the sale
		a purchase discount may be given to provide an incentive for faster payment. a discount is the amount will be deducted from the invoice price if payment is made within the allowed discount period
		for example, a 2% discount if paid in 10 days is stated as 2/10
	in this example the full terms are 2/10, n/30; this means the buyer can take a 2% discount if paid in 10 days, otherwise the full amount is due in 30 days

Purchase Discount Example
	Assume Kali purchased 200 cameras on account with the price of $42,000 and returned 20 cameras which are worth of $4,200. The sell provide the cash discount with the terms 2/10, n/30. Kali made payment within the discount period.
		Payment due before discount: $42,000 - $4,200 returns = $37,800
		Discount: $37,800 * 2% = $756
		Net cash payment: $37,800 - $756 = $37,044
	Kali records the following journal entry:
		Accounts payable (-L)		37,800
		  Inventory (-A)		  756
		  Cash (-A)			  37,044
		to record payment for 180 cameras within the discount period


Accounting for Sales of Merchandise (Perpetual System)
Requires two journal entries
	Record Sales Revenue
		Credit sales revenue and either
			Debit cash if the sale is for cash, or
			Debit Accounts receivable if the sale is on account
	Record Cost of Goods Sold
		Debit Cost of Goods Sold and credit inventory
	
	Kali Company sells 30 cameras on account for $500 each
	Record Sales Revenue
		Accounts receivable (+AR)		15,000
		  Sales revenue (+R, +SE)		  15,000
		to record the sale of 30 cameras
	Record Cost of Goods Sold
	($42,000 - $4,200 - $756) / (200 - 20) = $205.80 each
		Cost of goods sold (+E, -SE)		6,174
		  Inventory				  6,174
		to record the cost of 30 cameras sold

Sales Returns and Allowances
	Sales returns also require two journal entries
		Assume Kali had 5 cameras returned from the previous sale

		Sales returns and allowances (+XR, -SE)			2,500
		  Accounts receivable (-A)				  2,500
		to record the return of 5 cameras with a unit sales price of $500

		Inventory (+A)						1,029
		  Cost of Goods Sold (-E, +SE)				  1,029
		to record the return of 5 cameras with a unit cost of $205.80

	Sakes allowances only require the first journal entry because the goods are not returned

Sales Discount
	Assume Kali sold the cameras on account with the terms 2/10, n/30 and receives payment with the discount period
		Amount due before discount: $15,000 - $2,500 returns = $12,500
		Discount: $12,500 * 2% = $12,250
		Net cash received: $12,500 - $250 = $12,250
	Kali records the following journal entry:
	Cash (+A)			12,250
	Sales discount (+XR, -SE)	250
	  Accounts receivable (-A)	  12,500
	to record cash received and sales discount for 30 cameras within the discount period

Net Sales
	Sales are returns and allowances and sales discounts are contra-revenue accounts with a normal debit balance
	Subtracted from gross sales revenue to yield net sales reported on the income statement

	Gross sales revenue - sales returns and allowances - sales discounts = net sales

Gross Profit Percentage
	Gross Profit Percentage = Gross profit on sales / net sales
	Gross Profit on sales = Net sales - cost of goods sold
	Indicates the rate of profit on the goods a company sells before all other costs of running the business are considered
	Key measure of profitability followed by analysts

Return on Sales Ratio
	Return on sales ratio = net income / net sales
	Reveals the net income earned on each dollar of sales
	Subtracts all costs from net sales, not just the cost of goods sold
