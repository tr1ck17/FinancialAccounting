Long-Lived Assets
	Three categories of long-lived assets
		Plant Assets
		Intangible Assets
		Natural Resources
Plant Assets
	Plant assets refer to a company's long-lived property, plant, and equipment
		Land
		Buildings
		Equipment
		Furniture and fixtures
Intangible Assets
	Intangible assets are economic resources that benefit the company but lack physical substance
		Copyrights
		Trademarks
		Patents
		Franchises
Natural Resources
	Natural resources are resources supplied by nature
		Timber stands
		Mineral deposits
		Oil and gas deposits
Long-Lived Assets Summary
	Asset Category				Examples			Term for the Periodic Write-Off to Expense
	Plant assets			Buildings, equipment, tools, vehicles			Depreciation
	Intangible Assets		Patents, copyrights, leaseholds, franchises		Amortization
	Natural Resources		Timber, coal, oil and gas reserves, mineral deposits	Depletion

Accounting for Long-Lived Assets
	Accounting Issues:
		1. Account for the acquisition cost
		2. Allocate the asset's cost to expense over time
		3. Account for future expenditures related to the assets
		4. Account for the disposal of assets

Measuring Acquisition Cost
	Long-loved assets are initially recorded at their acquisition cost
	Also called the historical cost
	includes the cash paid to acquire the asset and get it ready for its intended use
		Sales taxes, freight charges, installation, testing

Package Purchases
	The total cost of assets purchased as a group is allocated to the individual assets because they may have different accounting and reporting requirements
	The allocation is based on relative market value or appraisal values
	For instance, Smith & Sons purchased a freight terminal that included land, a building, and equipment is $60,000, $120,000, and $20,000, respectively. How to allocate the $190,000 acquisition price among the three assets

Nature of Depreciation
	Systematic allocation of the cost of plant assets to the periods the assets are used in revenue generating activities
		Depreciation is not intended to measure changes in the asset's market value
		Land is not depreciated because it is considered to have an unlimited useful life

Calculating Depreciation Expense
	Calculating depreciation requires:
		1. Cost of the asset
		2. Estimated useful life of the asset to the firm
		3. Estimated salvage value when the asset is sold or removed from service
		4. Depreciation method:
			straight line
			declining balance
			units-of-production

Straight-Line Method
	Annual Depreciation = (Acquisition cost - Salvage value) / Estimated useful life
	Assume equipment acquisition cost of $5,000; three-year useful life; $500 salvage value

	($5,000 - $500) / 3 years = $1,500 per year

	Depreciation expense (+E, -SE)			$1,500
	   Accumulated depreciation (+XA, -A)		   $1,500

Disposal of Plant Assets
	To record a sale or retirement:
		1. Remove asset's original cost
		2. Remove accumulated depreciation
		3. Record proceeds
		4. Record any gain or loss
			Difference between proceeds received and book value
	Plant assets can also be exchanged for another plan asset
		Involves more complicated accounting not covered in this textbook

Disposals of Plant Assets Example
	A vehicle with an acquisition cost of $20,000 and accumulated depreciation of $18,000 is sold for $3,500

	3. Cash(+A)					3,500
	2. Accumulated depreciation (+XA, -A)		18,000
	1.    Vehicles (-A)				     20,000
	4.    Gain on sale (+Gain, +SE)			     1,500

	If the vehicle was sold for $1,500, step 4 would be a debit to Loss on sale for $500

Intangible assets benefit a company's operations but do not have a physical substance
	Patents - an exclusive privilege granted to an inventor for a period of 20 years
	Copyrights - protects an owner against unauthorized use of a written work, recorded work, or artwork for the life of the author plus 70 years
	Franchises - exclusive rights to operate or sell a specific brand of product in a given geographic area
	Trademarks - the right to use certain terms, names, or symbols
	Goodwill - the amount paid by a company for another company above the identifiable net assets of the acquired company

Accounting for Intangible Assets
	Intangible assets acquired from outside entities are recorded at their acquisition cost
	Intangible assets with a limited life:
		Amortize over the expected useful life generally using the straight-line method with no salvage value
		The journal entry debits amortization expense and credits the intangible asset account - there is no accumulated amortization account
	Intangible assets with an indefinite life:
		Goodwill
		Not amortized
	Research and development expenditures are expensed as incurred (not capitalized as an intangible asset)

Natural Resources
	Resources supplied by nature, such as standing timber, iron ore, coal, gold, and silver
	Recorded on the balance sheet at acquisition cost plus exploration and development costs
	Depletion is recorded as natural resources are removed using a method similar to the units-of-production method for plant assets
		The journal entry debits depletion expense and credits accumulated depletion

Analyzing Assets
	The ability to use a firm's assets efficiently and effectively is one sign of a well-managed company
		Return on assets measures how much profit a company is generating from its assets:

		Return on assets = Net income / Average total assets
	Asset turnover measures how effectively a company uses its assets to generate sales:

		Asset turnover = Net sales / Average total assets
